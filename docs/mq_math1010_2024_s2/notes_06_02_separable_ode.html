  • 8.3: Separable Equations

The General Solution includes arbitrary constants and we use the initial conditions to determine the values of these constants.

The arbitrary constants are constants of integration \[ F(x) = \int f(x) dx + C \]

This is explained through the examples below.

\begin{align*} y'(t) &= f(t) \\ \Rightarrow y(t) &= y(t_0) + \int_{t_0}^t f(u) du \end{align*}

By the fundamental theorem of calculus, and since \(y' = f\),

\begin{align*} y(t) - y(t_0) &= \int_{t_0}^t y'(u) du \\ &= \int_{t_0}^t f(u) du \end{align*}
\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} y'(t) &= t^2 \\ y(1) &= 3 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

To solve this equation using the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, we calculate

\begin{align*} y(t) &= y(1) + \int_1^t f(u) du \\ &= 3 + \int_1^t u^2 du \\ &= 3 + \left.\frac{u^3}{3}\right|_1^t \\ &= 3 + \frac{t^3}{3} - \frac{1}{3} \\ &= \frac{8}{3} + \frac{t^3}{3}. \end{align*}

We can check the answer solves the ODE by differentiating: \[ \frac{d}{dt} \left(\frac{8}{3} + \frac{t^3}{3}\right) = t^2. \]

For the initial condition, \[ \left.\left(\frac{8}{3} + \frac{t^3}{3}\right)\right|_{t=1} = \frac{8}{3} + \frac{1}{3} = 3. \]

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} z''(t) &= -g \\ z(0) &= z_0 \\ z'(0) &= v_0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

To apply the fundamental theorem of calculus, first let \(v = z'\). Then \(v' = (z')' = z'' = -g\) and \(v(0) = z'(0) = v_0\). Thus by the fundamental theorem of calculus,

\begin{align*} v(t) &= v(0) + \int_{0}^t v'(u) du \\ &= v(0) + \int_{0}^t -g du \\ &= v(0) - gt \\ &= v_0 - gt \end{align*}

Then using \(z' = v = v_0 - gt\) and \(z(0) = z_0\) we obtain

\begin{align*} z(t) &= z(0) + \int_{0}^t z'(u) du \\ &= z(0) + \int_{0}^t v(u) du \\ &= z_0 + \int_{0}^t v_0 - gu du \\ &= z_0 + v_0 \int_{0}^t du - g\int_{0}^t u du \\ &= z_0 + v_0 t - \frac{gt^2}{2} \end{align*}

Let's verify that this is indeed the solution. First

\begin{align*} z' &= \frac{d}{dt} \left(z_0 + v_0 t - \frac{gt^2}{2}\right) \\ &= v_0 - gt \\ \end{align*}

and \[ z'' = (v_0 - gt)' = -g \]

so that \(z\) does indeed solve the ODE. Second, for the initial conditions,

\begin{align*} z(0) &= z_0 + v_0 \times 0 - \frac{g \times 0^2}{2} = z_0 \\ z'(0) &= v_0 - g \times 0 = v_0. \end{align*}

A separable ODE is an ODE of the form \[ f(y) y' = g(t) \]

The variables have been separated into \(y\) variables on the left and \(t\) variables on the right. Such equations can be solved by substitution.

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} y' &= 3 y \\ y(0) &= 4 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

For \(y \neq 0\) we may write \[ \frac{1}{y} y' = 3 \]

This is of the form \(f(y) y' = g(t)\) with \(f(y) = \frac{1}{y}\) and \(g(t) = 3\). Integrating both sides with respect to \(t\) gives \[ \int \frac{1}{y} y' dt = \int 3 dt = 3t + C. \]

Letting \(u = y(t)\) on the left hand side, \(du = y' dt\) and so \[ \int \frac{1}{y} y' dt = \int \frac{1}{u} du = \ln |u| = \ln |y|. \]

Thus

\begin{align*} \ln |y| &= \int \frac{1}{y} y' dt \\ &= 3t + C \\ \end{align*}

Taking exponentials, \[ \lvert y \rvert = e^{3t + C} = e^C e^{3t}, \] which we can write as \[ y = \pm e^C e^{3t} = A e^{3t} \] where \(A = \pm e^C\).

The general solution is thus \[ y = A e^{3t} \] with \(A\) an arbitrary constant.

The initial condition allows us to solve for \(A\): \[ 4 = y(0) = Ae^{3 \times 0} = A. \]

Finally then, the solution is \[ y(t) = 4 e^{3t}. \]

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} y' &= (x^2 - 4)(3y + 2) \\ y(0) &= -2 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

Write the ODE as \[ \frac{y'}{3y+2} = x^2 - 4 \] which is a separable equation.

Integrating both sides with respect to \(x\) gives

\begin{align*} \int \frac{y'}{3y+2} dx &= \frac{x^3}{3} - 4x + C \\ &= \frac{x^3}{3} - 4x + C \end{align*}

For the integral on the left hand side, let \(u = 3y + 2\) so that \(du = 3y' dx\) and hence \(y' dx = \frac{1}{3} du\). Then

\begin{align*} \int \frac{y'}{3y+2} dx &= \int \frac{1}{u} \frac{1}{3} du \\ &= \frac{1}{3} \ln|u| \\ &= \frac{1}{3} \ln |3y + 2| \end{align*}

Therefore we get \[ \frac{1}{3} \ln |3y + 2| = \frac{x^3}{3} - 4x + C \]

Multiplying both sides by \(3\) gives \[ \ln |3y + 2| = x^3 - 12x + 3C = x^3 - 12x + B \] where \(B = 3C\).

Taking exponentials on both sides \[ \lvert 3y + 2\rvert = e^{x^3 - 12 x + B} \] hence

\begin{align*} 3y + 2 &= \pm e^{x^3 - 12x + B} \\ &= \pm e^B e^{x^3 - 12x} \\ &= A e^{x^3 - 12x} \end{align*}

where \(A = \pm e^B\).

Solving for \(y\) we obtain the general solution \[ y = \frac{-2 + A e^{x^3 - 12 x}}{3} \]

We determine the value of the constant \(A\) from the initial condition:

\begin{align*} -2 &= y(0) = \left.\frac{-2 + A e^{x^3 - 12 x}}{3}\right|_{x=0} \\ &= \frac{-2 + A e^{0^3 - 12 \times 0}}{3} \\ &= \frac{-2 + A}{3} \end{align*}

and hence \(A = -4\). The final solution is therefore \[ y = \frac{-2 - 4 e^{x^3 - 12 x}}{3} \]

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} p' &= 5p(3-p) \\ p(0) &= 1 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

This is a separable equation of the form, \[ \frac{p'}{p(3-p)} = 5 \]

To integrate this we write \[ \frac{1}{p(3-p)} = \frac{1}{3} \left(\frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{3-p}\right) \].

Then integrating gives

\begin{align*} \int \frac{1}{p(3-p)} dp &= \int \frac{1}{3} \left(\frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{3-p}\right) dp \\ &= \frac{1}{3} \ln |p| - \frac{1}{3} \ln |3-p| \\ &= \frac{1}{3} \ln \left\lvert\frac{p}{3-p}\right\rvert \end{align*}

Thus

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{3} \ln \left\lvert\frac{p}{3-p}\right\rvert &= \int \frac{1}{p(3-p)} dp \\ &= \int \frac{1}{p(3-p)} p' dt \\ &= \int 5 dt \\ &= 5t + C \end{align*}

Thus \[ \ln \left\lvert \frac{p}{3-p}\right\rvert = 15 t + D \] where \(D = 3C\).

Taking exponentials,

\begin{align*} \frac{p}{3-p} &= \pm e^{15t + D} = B e^{15t} \end{align*}

where \(B = \pm e^D\).

Multiplying both sides by \(3-p\) gives \[ p = B e^{15t}(3-p) \] which rearranges to give \[ p(1 + B e^{15t}) = 3 Be^{15t} \] and hence

\begin{align*} p(t) &= \frac{3B e^{15t}}{1 + B e^{15t}} \\ &= \frac{3}{\tfrac{1}{B} e^{-15t} + 1} \end{align*}

From the initial condition \(p(0) = 1\) we can solve for \(B\): \[ 1 = p(0) = \frac{3}{\tfrac{1}{B} + 1} \] hence \(B = \tfrac{1}{2}\). Thus finally \[ p(t) = \frac{3}{1 + 2 e^{-15t}} \]
