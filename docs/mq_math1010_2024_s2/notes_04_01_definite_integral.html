Let \([a, b]\) be a closed interval. Given an positive integer \(n\), let \(\Delta x = \frac{b-a}{n}\). The regular partition of \([a, b]\) into \(n\) equally spaces intervals is the set of intevals, \[ I_i = [x_{i-1}, x_i] \] for \(i = 1, \dots, n\) and where \(x_i = a + i \Delta x\) for \(i = 0, \dots, n\).

Let \([a, b] = [2, 5]\) and let \(n = 3\). Then \(\Delta x = \frac{5-2}{3} = 1\). We have

\begin{align*} x_0 &= 2 + 0 \times 1 = 2 \\ x_1 &= 2 + 1 \times 1 = 3 \\ x_2 &= 2 + 2 \times 1 = 4 \\ x_3 &= 2 + 3 \times 1 = 5 \end{align*}

The intervals are

\begin{align*} I_1 &= [2, 3] \\ I_2 &= [3, 4] \\ I_3 &= [4, 5] \end{align*}

Notice that the length of each interval is \[ x_i - x_{i-1} = (a + i\Delta x) - (a + (i-1)\Delta x) = \Delta x. \]

Let \([a, b] = [2, 5]\) and let \(n = 6\). Then \(\Delta x = \frac{5-2}{6} = 1/2\). We have

\begin{align*} x_0 &= 2 + 0 \times 1/2 = 2 \\ x_1 &= 2 + 1 \times 1/2 = 2.5 \\ x_2 &= 2 + 2 \times 1/2 = 3 \\ x_3 &= 2 + 3 \times 1/2 = 3.5 \\ x_4 &= 2 + 1 \times 1/2 = 4 \\ x_5 &= 2 + 2 \times 1/2 = 4.5 \\ x_6 &= 2 + 3 \times 1/2 = 5 \end{align*}

The intervals are

\begin{align*} I_1 &= [2, 2.5] \\ I_2 &= [2.5, 3] \\ I_3 &= [3, 3.5] \\ I_4 &= [3.5, 4] \\ I_5 &= [4, 4.5] \\ I_6 &= [4.5, 5] \end{align*}

Let \(f : [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}\) be a continuous function. Let \(x_0, \cdots, x_n\) be the endpoints of the intervals \(I_i = [x_{i-1}, x_i]\) of the regular partition. Let

\begin{align*} m_i &= \min \{f(x) : x \in I_i\} \\ M_i &= \max \{f(x) : x \in I_i\}. \end{align*}

Let \(f : [2, 5] \to \mathbb{R}\) be defined by \(f(x) = x + 4\). Since \(f'(x) = 1 > 0\), \(f\) is increasing. Thus the minimum on \(I_i\) occurs at the left end point \[ m_i = \min\{x + 4 : x_{i-1} \leq x \leq x_i\} = x_{i-1} + 4. \]

The maximum occurs at the right end point, \[ M_i = \max \{x + 4 : x_{i-1} \leq x \leq x_i\} = x_i + 4. \]

Now let \(n=3\). From an example above, we obtained \(x_0 = 2\), \(x_1 = 3\), \(x_2 = 4\), and \(x_3 = 5\). Then we have

\begin{align*} m_1 &= x_0 + 4 = 6 \\ m_2 &= x_1 + 4 = 7 m_3 &= x_2 + 4 = 8 \end{align*}

and

\begin{align*} M_1 &= x_1 + 4 = 7 \\ M_2 &= x_2 + 4 = 8 M_3 &= x_3 + 4 = 9 \end{align*}

The minimum sum for \(f\) is

\begin{align*} m_f(n) &= m_1 \Delta x + m_2 \Delta x + \dots + m_{n-1} \Delta x + m_n \Delta x \\ &= (m_1 + \dots + m_n) \Delta x. \end{align*}

The maximum sum is

\begin{align*} M_f(n) &= M_1 \Delta x + M_2 \Delta x + \dots + M_{n-1} \Delta x + M_n \Delta x \\ &= (M_1 + \dots + M_n) \Delta x. \end{align*}

The area under the graph of \(f\) satisfies \[ m_f(n) \leq \text{Area} \leq M_f(n). \]

Let \(f : [2, 5] \to \mathbb{R}\) be defined by \(f(x) = x + 4\) and \(n=3\). Using \(\Delta x = (5-2)/3 = 1\) and the values of \(m_i, M_i\) obtained above,

\begin{align*} m_f(3) &= (m_1 + m_2 + m_3) \Delta x \\ &= (6 + 7 + 8) \times 1\\ &= 21. \end{align*}

and

\begin{align*} M_f(3) &= (M_1 + M_2 + M_3) \Delta x \\ &= (7 + 8 + 9) \times 1\\ &= 24. \end{align*}

Let \(f : [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}\) be a continuous function. Then the following limits exist and are equal, \[ \lim_{n \to \infty} m_f(n) = \lim_{n\to\infty} M_f(n). \]

The definite integral is \[ \int_a^b f(x) dx = \lim_{n\to\infty} m_f(n) = \lim_{n\to\infty} M_f(n). \]

Roughly speaking, the integral of \(f\) is obtained by adding up all the values of \(f\) over shorter and shorter intervals. In the limit, we get the integral, which we think of as sort of sum of the values of \(f\) over the continuously variying values of \(x \in [a, ]\). Sometimes the integral is referred to as a continuous sum.

Another interpretation is as the area under the graph of \(f\). We observed above that\[ m_f(n) \leq \text{Area} \leq M_f(n). \] Thus by the squeeze theorem, \[ \int_a^b f(x) dx = \text{Area}. \]

Let \(f(x) = x\) and \([a, b] = [0, 1]\). For any \(n\), \(\Delta n = \tfrac{1-0}{n} = \frac{1}{n}\). Then \[ x_i = 0 + i \Delta x = \frac{i}{n}. \]

Since \(f\) is increasing, the minimum over \(I_i\) occurs at the left hand endpoint, \(x_{i-1}\) and the maximum occurs at the right hand endpoint, \(x_i\). Therefore

\begin{align*} m_i &= f(x_{i-1}) = \frac{i-1}{n} \\ M_i &= f(x_i) = \frac{i}{n} \end{align*}

The minimum sum is

\begin{align*} m_f(n) &= \Delta x [m_1 + \dots + m_n] \\ &= \frac{1}{n} \left[\frac{0}{n} + \frac{1}{n} + \dots + \frac{n-1}{n}\right] \\ &= \frac{1}{n} \frac{0 + 1 + \dots + n-1}{n} \\ &= \frac{\tfrac{(n-1)(n-2)}{2}}{n^2} \\ &= \frac{1}{2} - \frac{3}{2n} + \frac{1}{n^2} \end{align*}

Taking the limit \(n\to\infty\),

\begin{align*} \int_0^1 x dx &= \lim_{n\to\infty} m(n) \\ &= \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{2} - \frac{3}{2n} + \frac{1}{n^2} \\ &= \frac{1}{2}. \end{align*}

The maximum sum is similar.
