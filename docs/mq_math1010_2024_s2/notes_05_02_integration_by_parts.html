  • 7.1: Integration by Parts

By the product rule, \[ \left(xe^x\right)' = e^x + x e^x. \]

Therefore by the fundamental theorem of calculus,

\begin{align*} \int e^x + x e^x dx &= \int \left(xe^x\right)' dx \\ &= xe^x + C. \end{align*}

By linearity we also have that \[ \int e^x + xe^x dx = \int e^x dx + \int x e^x dx = e^x + \int xe^x dx. \]

Putting these two equations together we get \[ e^x + \int x e^x dx = x e^x + C. \]

Solving for \(\int x e^x dx\) then gives \[ \int x e^x dx = x e^x - e^x + C. \]

Let's check this is correct by differentiating using the product rule: \[ \frac{d}{dx} \left(x e^x - e^x + C\right) = e^x + x e^x - e^x = x e^x. \]

\[ \int fg' dx = fg - \int f' g dx \]

By the product rule, \[ (fg)' = f'g + fg'. \]

Therefore \[ fg' = (fg)' - f' g. \]

Integrating and using the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus gives \[ \int fg' dx = \int (fg)' - f'g dx = fg - \int f' g dx. \]

Let's put everything together into a rule we can apply. For a function \(u(x)\) let us write \[ du = u' dx. \]

\[ \int u dv = uv - \int v du \]

From the product rule and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus,

\begin{align*} \int u dv &= \int u v' dx \\ &= \int (uv)' - u' v dx \\ &= uv - \int v u' dx \\ &= uv - \int v du. \end{align*}

Calculate the integral \[ \int x e^x dx \]

Let \(u = x\) and \(dv = e^x dx\). Then \(du = dx\) and \[ v = \int dv = \int e^x dx = e^x. \]

Thus by the theorem,

\begin{align*} \int x e^x dx &= \int u dv = uv - \int v du \\ &= x e^x - \int e^x dx \\ &= x e^x - e^x + C \end{align*}

agreeing with what we've already obtained above.

Calculate the integral \[ \int \frac{\ln x}{x^3} dx. \]

Let \(u = \ln x\) and \(dv = \frac{1}{x^3} dx\). Then \(du = \frac{1}{x}\) and using the rule \(\int x^n dx = \tfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}\) with \(n=-3\) we get \[ v = \int dv = \int x^{-3} dx = \frac{x^{-2}}{-2} = -\frac{1}{2x^2}. \]

Thus by the theorem,

\begin{align*} \int \frac{\ln x}{x^x} dx &= \int u dv = uv - \int v du \\ &= -\frac{\ln x}{2x^2} - \int \left(-\frac{1}{2x^2}\right) \frac{1}{x} dx \\ &= -\frac{\ln x}{2x^2} + \int \frac{1}{2x^3} dx \\ &= -\frac{\ln x}{2x^2} - \frac{1}{4x^2} + C. \end{align*}

Let's check this by differentiating:

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx} \left(-\frac{\ln x}{2x^2} - \frac{1}{4x^2}\right) &= -\frac{1/x}{2x^2} + \frac{\ln x}{x^3} + \frac{1}{2 x^2} \\ &= \frac{\ln x}{x^3}. \end{align*}

When integrating by parts, we must choose which part of the integrand is \(u\) and which part is \(dv\). There is a bit of an art to it, but there are also some helpful guidelines.

The following gives a list of precedence for which function should be \(u\).

  • I​nverse trig: \(\arcsin, \arccos\) etc.
  • L​ogarithmic \(\ln x\), \(\log_2 x\)
  • A​lgebraic \(x^2, \sqrt{x}\) etc.
  • T​rigonometric \(\sin, \cos\) etc.
  • E​xponential \(e^x, 2^x\) etc.

If there is an inverse trig function, that should be \(u\). If not, then if there is a logarithm, it should \(u\). If not, \(u\) should be an algebraic expression and so on.

Calculate the integral \[ \int x \ln x dx \]

The integrand is a product of the A​lgebraic expression \(x\) and the L​ograithm \(\ln x\) . Applying the ILATE rule, we set \(u = \ln x\) and \(dv = x dx\). Then \(du = \tfrac{1}{x} dx\) and \(v = \tfrac{x^2}{2}\). Integrating by parts we get

\begin{align*} \int x \ln x dx &= \int u dv = uv - \int v du \\ &= \frac{x^2\ln x}{2} - \int \tfrac{x^2}{2} \frac{1}{x} dx \\ &= \frac{x^2\ln x}{2} - \int \tfrac{x}{2} dx \\ &= \frac{x^2 \ln x}{2} - \frac{x^2}{4} + C. \end{align*}

Checking our answer by differentiating,

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx} \left(\frac{x^2 \ln x}{2} - \frac{x^2}{4} + C\right) &= x \ln x + \frac{x}{2} - \frac{x}{2} \\ &= x \ln x. \end{align*}

Calculate the integral \[ \int \arcsin (x) dx \]

There is only one function there so it seems like we can't integrate by parts. In fact we can! Following ILATE we have an I​nverse trig function so we let \(u = \arcsin x\). But then what is \(dv\)? It's just \(dv = dx\)! Thus we have \(du = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}dx\) and \(v = x\). Integrating by parts we get

\begin{align*} \int \arcsin(x) dx &= \int u dx = uv - \int v du \\ &= x \arcsin(x) - \int \frac{x}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} dx \end{align*}

Making the substitution \(y = 1-x^2\) in the last integral gives \(dy = -2 x dx\). Thus

\begin{align*} \int \arcsin(x) &= x \arcsin (x) + \frac{1}{2} \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{y}} dy \\ &= x \arcsin(x) + \sqrt{y} \\ &= x \arcsin(x) + \sqrt{1-x^2} + C. \end{align*}

Checking our answer by differentiation:

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx} \left(x\arcsin(x) + \sqrt{1-x^2} + C\right) &= \arcsin(x) + \frac{x}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} + \frac{1}{2\sqrt{1-x^2}} \times (-2x) \\ &= \arcsin(x) + \frac{x}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} - \frac{x}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \\ &= \arcsin(x). \end{align*}
